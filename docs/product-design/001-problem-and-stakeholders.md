# Humbert

Meet Humbert, a light and friendly analytics service for the cultural and youth sector. It helps people ask better questions of their data, and get useful answers faster. Humbert is a portmanteau of `human` and `camembert`, we use to honor the French habit of calling a pie chart a camembert. 

## The problem, and who has it

Humbert exists because many organisations in the cultural sector, including government departements, have questions they cannot answer quickly enough. They collect data for reports, subsidies, policy work, participation, audiences, activities, finances, or impact. But when a real question appears, the answer is often slow to produce.

The problem is not that there is no data.  
The problem is that turning data into a clear, trustworthy answer still takes too much effort.

A director wants to understand participation.  
A policy officer wants to prepare a briefing.  
A sector organisation wants to compare trends.  
A cabinet wants a quick answer before a meeting.  
An analyst can probably answer it, but only after finding the right data, checking definitions, writing queries, building a chart, and explaining the caveats.

Humbert tries to make that path shorter. By helping the analyst answer faster, clearer, and with less repetitive work.

>**Not:** “Here is a dashboard.”  
>**But:** “Here is a good first answer to the question you just asked.”

### The core idea

Humbert is _human_: it is not a self-service fantasy where everyone becomes a data expert. AI can help with exploration, summaries, charts, draft explanations, and follow-up questions. But the human analyst remains responsible for judgement, context, and trust. Humbert assumes there are people who know the data, understand the sector, and can validate the answer.

## The second promise: data should come back useful

Many organisations put effort into collecting and sharing data, but often get little back.

They fill in templates.  
They deliver reports.  
They send numbers upward.  
And what returns is often a static dashboard, a PDF, or nothing directly reusable.

Humbert should offer something more useful in return. It can start simply: better exports, clearer indicators, reusable summaries, and answers that organisations can use in their own work.

## Stakeholders

The simple stakeholder model ~ humbert has three main movements:

1. **A question comes in**  
    A decision-maker, policy officer, or sector partner needs an answer.
2. **A human analyst works with AI**  
    Humbert helps explore the data, draft charts, explain results, and make the answer usable.
3. **Value flows back**  
    The organisations that provided data get something useful in return: insight, enriched data, reusable indicators, or clearer feedback.

That is the ❤️ of Humbert:

> Faster answers for people with questions.  
> Better tools for humans who understand the data.  
> More value returned to the sector that provides the data.

## 1. The question-holder

This can be a director at a sector support org, a government policy officer, or a cabinet advisor. They have a concrete question, often under time pressure. What they want from Humbert:
- a faster route from question to answer;
- enough confidence to use the answer in a meeting, note, report, or decision.

## 2. The analyst

The analyst is someone who knows the data well. What they need is help with the repetitive parts: finding the right data, drafting queries, producing charts, writing explanations. What they need from Humbert:
- less plumbing;
- faster exploration;
- reproducible answers;
- control over definitions and interpretation;
- a tool that respects their expertise.

## 3. The data provider

This is the cultural organisation, local partner or service that produces and shares the data. What they want from Humbert:
- enriched data they can use again;
- less one-way reporting;
- more transparency about how their data is interpreted.

## 4. The data steward

This is someone responsible for definitions, indicators, data quality, and interpretation. They are responsible to make Humbert trustworthy.. They know that “participant”, “activity”, “organisation”, “reach”, or “engagement” can mean different things in different contexts. What they need from Humbert:
- control over domain knowledge;
- transparent assumptions;
- a way to prevent every answer from reinventing the same concepts.
